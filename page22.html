HISTORY OF SAMOKI

            Like any other place, Samoki has its own origin. Its story has been passed down from generation to generation by word of mouth. But its origin is still shrouded in mystery. Believe or not, even the elders have their own stories on how Samoki came to be.

            In the surviving stories, the place where Samoki is presently located was omnce a pagpag with no people. There lived three brothers in Pespes (Caluttit) named Chongyonen, Lingtan and Amoning. One day they found out that their pregnant pig was lost. As the eldest, Chongyonen set off to find the pig; Lingtan and Amoning decided to follow their eldest brother after he didn’t come for lunch. After hours of looking, Chongyonen found the pig in Feshnakhan (Khawana). He also found that the pig bore piglets there. 

            The two younger brothers found Chongyonen, and then the three of them decided to live there because it had plenty of water and the place was favorable for settlement. The brothers divided the place into three and ruled over their respective territories. Amoning, the last child had the widest land because he was the strongest. 

            Today, Samoki has three main sitios: Khefa, Khawana and Sadlan. Khefa is subdivided into four smaller sitios of Pawek, Marivic, Khakhangar and Salin. Khawana is subdivided into Attor, Tochey and Engab. Sadlan covers the five smaller sitios of Fano-oy, Fangyaw, Kalonglong, Finew and Tangachag. The village also have the atos of Nurgar, Nakawang, Khawana and Kongofan. Traditionally, “Kidla” and “Paingan” are the names of Samoki. “Kidla-a” is usually used in songs. One source of livelihood of the villagers long ago was to make earthen jars baked in makeshift ovens which produced smoke profusely from the baking of the jars. 

            A foreigner passing by the village asked a villager what the name of the place was. The foreigner kept repeating the word “smoky”. The villager did not understand what the foreigner was saying so he just nodded and said “Samoki”. That was how Samoki got its name. 

            War memories are best remembered by the elders. According to them, the Japanese never harmed a single native when they came to Bontoc during the war. That was because Dr. Hilary Clapp, the first Filipino-Igorot governor of Mountain Province, maintained peaceful relations with the soldiers. A couple named Fe-et and Tukakaw, then the wealthiest people in Samoki, even gave 18 granaries of rice and offered their house for the Japanese to use. 

            At present, Samoki has a population of 2,938 individuals dwelling in a land area of 1,379.39 hectares.
