HISTORY OF CAN-EO

            Barangay Can-eo cradles the sitios of Chaowang, Fangkag, Lagwab, Suyo and chapyosen, all within a land area of 2,941 hectares.

            A farmer who was once missing a pregnant pig found it at the area where the Papattay currently stands. This event led people to follow the pig and live there and built what is now the main settlement of can-eo. Before that, they lived in Hummad, now a patch of rice fields viewed from across the river. 

            The movement of the people who ended up in Hummad has a longer story which started in Lufokâ€“ their original settlemtn at the border of Tocucan and anabel. They grew tired of life there and moved out. When they reached the intersection of the two rivers from Central Bontoc and Caneo, half of them went a separate way and settled at Diwdiw. They would later become the people of Tocucn. The other half continued their journey until they reached Amfag-ay. At that point, some of them wanted to go on their own and they journeyed until they found Samoki. 

            While some remained in Amfag-ay, other went to Falittoy. These two settlements were near each other such that the plaing of gongs in Amfag-ay could be heard in Falittoy and vice versa. Those in Falittoy relocate further. Half of them would go to Talubin and the other half to Lias, Barlig. The people in Amfag-ay on the other hand would transfer to Hummad, from where the straying pig led them to and what is now the main settlement of Can-eo. 

            Separated from other sitios by a few kilometers walk along the rice fields and river shore is the settlement of Chapyuen. An epidemic in the main settlement drove eight households to leave and go up the mountains to a place called Laylaya. The epidemic spread and caused people to vomit blood and die, claiming two to three people in a day. The eight households who survived in Laylaya remained until they grew to 11 households. Since Laylaya is on top of a mountain, it was difficult to go down to their rice fields and back up again to their homes. A man named Ongog suggested to his fellow villagers that they resettle near their source of livelihood. Agreeing with the soundness of the suggestion, the 11 households relocated lower down the mountain, nearer their rice fields. Now, the households of Chapyusen number to 20 (excluding those who have emigrated). 

            Although legend has it that the people of Can-eo were cursed by Lumawig not to increase in number, their current population of 632 individuals in contrast to earlier statistics attest to otherwise.
