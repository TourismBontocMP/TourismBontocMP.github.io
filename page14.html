HISTORY OF GONOGON

            Gonogonians claim their roots in Palale, a place located in the eastern side of the Chico River between present day of Alab and Gonogon. Palalae served as their place of refuge from tribal wars between Bontoc and Sagada. Due to aggressive assaults waged by the warriors of Deydey, the early settlers relocated to Mabika, a place near Sagada. However, Bontoc warriors got involved into tribal wars with Sagada. They had to pass by Mabika to reach Sagada making the new settlement of impending wars; they again transferred to Matiim, finding out a little too late that the place lied in the warpath of warriors. Eventually, they reached Palale and finally found peace there.

            However, the Gonogonian exodus did not end there. According to the history orally handed down from the past generations of settlers, there was once a couple who lived in Palale. They had a pregnant pig that wandered off and headed to a place unknown to the people then. The pig reached a cogon-covered plain where it found itself resting after days of walking a long distance. Not long after it took a break from walking, the pig eventually gave birth to 7 piglets. 

            The man in search for his lost pig traveled through the mountainside following a southbound trail. Soon he found his pig together with the seven piglets at a place called Laglagan. (At present, one can find Gonogon Elementary School located in that particular area where the pigs were found.) He noticed that the place was abundant with rattan and cogon. He decided to build a makeshift pen to protect the pigs from the harsh rainfall and sun’s heat. 

            When he decided to go back to Palale, he found it hard to bring with him the pig and its piglets. He then started constructing a temporary shelter for himself with the use of cogon and rattan. His family back home learned of the discovery of the new place and followed him to Legab. The whole family decided to stay there as the place proved better than their old village in Palale. Later on, several members of their tribe joined the as they permanently settled in the area.

            As the settlement became more populated others went to create the communities in Sitio Sumbagan and Sayudang. The three sitios: Legab Sumbagan and Sayudang comprise the Barangay Gonogon at present. Gonogon’s 768 hectares territory has 199 families with 696 individuals
